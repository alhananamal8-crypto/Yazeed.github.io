Background

Overall context

QReward is a mobile app that helps businesses to boost sales and increase customer retention. The founder wants businesses to be able to see the insights and the analysis of users interacting with the app. As a product designer, the task was to design the dashboard for businesses so that they can see the sales and users insights through a web based dashboard. QReward is app based, but business dashboard is web based. This is a 6-week corporate project.

My role

I worked as full-time Product Designer doing everything from UX research to UI design to the development handoff phase: stakeholder interview, user interview, research studies, prototyping, and usability testing. Our design team had 2 people.

Problem statement

Businesses want to see data and facts. They also want to see insights to make decisions.

Therefore, the dashboard needs to cover 2 requirements: Informational and Analytical. Informational: Shows data and facts. Based on data and facts, the dashboard provides an analysis of this information and offers recommendations for solving a problem or making a business decision.

Solution

"There are several different types of dashboards: key performance indicator (KPI) dashboards, analytic dashboards, business intelligence (BI) dashboards, and productized application dashboards." (Daniel O’Sullivan, 2016). From the list of types of dashboards, I identified QReward dashboard belongs to productized application dashboards.

To design this type of dashboard, I followed "Application Distilling" from Daniel.

  • Step 1: Take a large whiteboard and write 3 words across the top: Features, Navigations, and Metrics
  • Step 2: Give the participants a Sharpie and 3 sticky notepads in different colours. Have them list out everything the application does in each of those categories, and then place each one on the board under its respective heading.
  • Step 3: Draw a large rectangle on the board to represent the dashboard with a section across the top or left-hand side for the navigation
  • Step 4: Place the links in the navigation area and the features and metrics in the main area. 
  • Step 5: Refine the final sticky notes left standing on the board to their basic elements. E.g. I included a calendar in the design, I needed to decide which level it should default to (day, week, or month).

As a last step, I mocked up "application distilling" wireframes based on the UI Design System, which was designed by me and the lead designer. Here is the story of how I came up with the design system for QReward.

Research Process

Stakeholder Interview

Why I chose this methodology: The founder had strong detailed concepts about the app. He strongly believed in his initial sketches and didn't want to spend money on research. Stakeholder interview is the only way to deeply understand his concepts to develop the final products based on his ideas while balancing the user centric design.

My lead designer and I carried stakeholder interviews 3-4 times a week to understand the nature of the business model. I realized business plays an important role in designing the product. I collected stakeholder answers, grouped problems and brainstormed solutions for each problem group. Below is the summary of stakeholder interviews:

Competitor Analysis

Why I chose this methodology: Dashboard is tough to design because it's a B2B product. Our research budget wasn't enough for business. Competitor Analysis saved us a lot of money. In addition, dashboard needs in-depth insights so methodology like survey won't fit in.

I researched all the dashboards that I daily use (Udemy, Medium, Quora, Shopify). I registered to other platforms (RBC) to learn about how their dashboards work.

Udemy

The thing I like the most about their dashboard is the "Create announcement" feature. Instructor can send email newsletters to all student databases once a week, 4 per month. I applied this feature to our design: Businesses can create announcements from the dashboard and these messages will be sent directly to the user app.

Medium

The thing I like the most about Medium's dashboard is the correspondence of 3 bar graphs. I took this as inspiration for our "Analytics" on the QReward dashboard.

Quora

RBC

Quora uses bar graphs wisely to display the numbers of views.

RBC uses line graphs over the time period. I decide to allow businesses to view their statistics over 7 days, last month, and every month.

Ideate solution

As mentioned above, to design this productized application dashboard, I followed "Application Distilling" from Daniel O’Sullivan.

Distilling Step 1

I opened a large whiteboard on Miro and write 3 words across the top: Features, Navigations, and Metrics. I used 3 sticky notepads in different colours. Have them list out everything the application does in each of those categories, and then place each one on the board under its respective heading.

Distilling Step 2,3,4,5

I drew a large rectangle on the board to represent the dashboard with a section across the top or left-hand side for the navigation. Place the links in the navigation area and the features and metrics in the main area. This step takes a considerable amount of time with lots of back and forth.

I worked on this step again and again 3 different times: First time with the lead designer, the second time with the founder, the third me alone.
